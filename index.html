Italian Pronunciation Guide for English Speakers

Italian has seven steady vowel phonemes and a mostly consistent set of consonant patterns. This guide uses IPA and notes a few regional variations: Standard Italian (based on Tuscan/Florentine), Northern Italian, Southern Italian (Neapolitan, Sicilian), and other local influences. Unlike English, Italian pronunciation is highly regular and spelling is closely tied to sound.

Stress and Accent Marks

Italian stress usually falls on the penultimate syllable. If not, it is marked with an accent (e.g., perch√© [perÀàke]). Final-stress words (like citt√† [t ÉitÀàta]) always carry a written accent. Italian has no vowel reduction: every vowel is pronounced clearly.

Regional Notes at a Glance

Standard/Tuscan: basis of official Italian; clear vowels and consonants.
Northern Italy: sometimes weak final vowels, "s" often voiced, less rolled r.
Southern Italy (Neapolitan, Sicilian): stronger open vowels, more trill, some unique intonations.
Roman speech: notable strong / É/ and doubled consonants.

Helpful Symbols and Conventions

IPA brackets [ ] show actual sound; slashes / / show phoneme categories. Double consonants are phonemic and must be pronounced distinctly (pala "shovel" vs palla "ball"). Accents indicate stress only when irregular. Vowels never reduce to schwa (unlike English).
